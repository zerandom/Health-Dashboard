Heart Rate Variability (HRV) is a measure of the variation in time between each heartbeat. This variation is controlled by a primitive part of the nervous system called the autonomic nervous system (ANS). The ANS has two branches: the sympathetic nervous system (fight or flight) and the parasympathetic nervous system (rest and digest). A higher HRV indicates greater dominance of the parasympathetic system, signaling that the body is recovering well, adapting to training, and is ready for further stress. Conversely, a lower HRV indicates that the sympathetic system is active, which can be due to training fatigue, psychological stress, illness, or poor sleep.

When monitoring HRV for athletes, the most important metric is not the absolute value on any given day, but rather the trend over time compared to a personal baseline. A normal daily fluctuation is expected, but when HRV drops significantly below the 30-day or 7-day rolling average, it is a clear physiological signal of accumulated stress debt. In these scenarios, high-intensity interval training (HIIT) or heavy strength training should be avoided, as the body lacks the capacity to adapt to the stimulus. Instead, the athlete should focus on active recovery, Zone 1 or Zone 2 aerobic work, and prioritizing sleep.

An acute drop in HRV that coincides with poor deep sleep or a late bedtime often indicates poor recovery from the previous day's load. If the athlete's 7-day average HRV drops below their 21-day average, this is known as a negative stress debt. If this trend continues for more than a few days, the athlete is at high risk of overtraining syndrome. In this state, even if the athlete feels subjectively energized, the objective data suggests a mandatory rest day or very light active recovery is required until the HRV returns to the baseline window.

Furthermore, it is well documented that alcohol consumption, late-night eating, and poor sleep efficiency drastically lower the next morning's HRV. If an athlete presents with a lower than normal HRV, checking the previous night's sleep duration and bedtime is the first step in diagnosing the root cause. If sleep was adequate but HRV is still low, the athlete may be fighting a subclinical infection or dealing with excessive non-training life stress.
