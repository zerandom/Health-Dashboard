Training load management is critical for preventing overtraining, mitigating injury risk, and ensuring optimal performance peaking. According to foundational strength and conditioning guidelines, including those from the NSCA, training load should be increased gradually. The "10% Rule" is a widely accepted guideline which states that weekly training volume or intensity should not increase by more than 10% from the previous week. Rapid spikes in acute training load, especially when the chronic training load (the 4-week average) is low, significantly increase the risk of injury.

The Banister Impulse-Response Model formalizes this by separating training effects into "Fitness" (positive, long-lasting adaptations) and "Fatigue" (negative, short-term physiological taxation). Your Training Stress Balance (TSB) is calculated as Fitness minus Fatigue. When TSB is heavily negative, the athlete is in a state of high fatigue. If TSB remains extremely negative for multiple weeks without a deload, the athlete enters a state of overreaching, which can degrade into overtraining syndrome. To manage this, athletes should maintain a weekly load ceiling and take a "deload week" every 3 to 6 weeks, where volume is reduced by 30-50% to allow fatigue to dissipate while fitness is maintained.

When determining daily intensity, it is recommended to polarize training. Approximately 80% of training time should be spent in Zone 1 and Zone 2 (low intensity, purely aerobic), which builds the mitochondrial base and has a low neurological cost. Only 20% of training time should be spent in high-intensity zones (Zone 4 and Zone 5). If an athlete's resting heart rate (RHR) is elevated or their HRV is suppressed, they should skip planned high-intensity sessions and instead default to Zone 1 or complete rest, as their body lacks the parasympathetic capacity to absorb high-stress stimuli.
